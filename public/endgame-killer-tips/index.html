Endgames Aren’t Just Grinding

Most Gambonanza matches end before the 6×6 board. But what separates high scores from average runs is how you close out the game when the board expands.

Verdict
The biggest enemy in endgames isn't the opponent's piece density — it's your own rush-to-finish mentality. 90% of comebacks don't come from outplaying — they come from the opponent making more mistakes in the endgame than you do.

1. Bishop Stalemate — Breaking Same-Color Deadlock

The Problem

Once the board expands to 6×6+, if both sides are left with same-color Bishops, you’re in a deadlock. Neither can capture the other.

Solutions

Three Ways to Break Through

Method A: Rook Force Entry

  • Rook dominates same-color Bishops — it’s not restricted by color
  • Place your Rook on the opposite color where the enemy Bishop can’t reach

Method B: Pawn Sacrifice for Space

  • Sacrifice 2+ Pawns consecutively to force the enemy Bishop off key squares
  • Rhythm: Pawn → Pawn → Bishop follows into the opened wing

Method C: Queen Cleanup

  • Queen ignores color restrictions — directly hunt the enemy Bishop
  • Reserve Gambit slots for Queen protection and repositioning
Bishop Stalemate Myth
Many players think Bishop stalemate is about who blinks first. Wrong. The correct solution is introducing a third color — Rook (colorless) or Queen (all colors). Trying to out-Bishop an opponent Bishop only drags the game forever.

2. King Hunt — Your Three-Turn Window

When the enemy King is exposed (fewer than 3 friendly pieces nearby), you have a 3-turn kill window.

Hunt Rhythm

TurnActionGoal
T0 (Exposure)Identify the Killing PatternConfirm adjacent squares to King are empty
T1Double ApproachMove two heavy hitters toward the King simultaneously
T2Gambit + ExecuteUse displacement Gambit to block King’s escape route
T3 (Window closes)Finish or RetreatIf King gets protected, pull back immediately
Common Mistake
Once the King gets protected (pieces repositioned nearby), retreat immediately. Don't force the kill. Most endgame throws happen because players keep chasing after the window closes.

3. Relic Timing — When to Open What

PhaseOpen TheseSave These
Early (3×3)Economy relicsCombat relics for mid-game
Mid (4×4~5×5)Gambit slot upgradesFinisher relics for endgame
Late (6×6+)All damage relicsEconomy relics are now worthless

Late Economy relics should be sold by after 5×5 — their peak value has passed and combat relics take priority.

4. Pawn Promotion Traps

When a Pawn promotes, it stops being a Pawn. This means:

  1. All Pawn Gambit bonuses disappear
  2. Any “if you control a Pawn” relic effects break
  3. Counterplay: If your opponent relies on Pawn Gambits, force their Pawns to promote — you’ll cripple their entire strategy
Pawn Trap in Action
Spot an opponent spamming Pawn Gambit? Leave a tempting Rook on their promotion path. Their Pawn greed-promotes, and you've just killed their Gambit engine. This is called the "Sacrifice Rook, Break Gambit" tactic.

5. Comeback Checklist

Preparation screen — check your relics and Gambits before the endgame push

When losing, run through this checklist in order:

  • How many of your pieces are still on the board?
  • How many unopened relics do you have?
  • Are your Gambit slots full?
  • Has the enemy King been exposed in the last 3 turns?
  • Is there a same-color Bishop stalemate you can exploit?

Most comebacks aren’t skill-based outplays — they’re methodical checklists that find your opponent’s blind spots.
